# Research methodology

**Bitcoin for the Arts Research** begins with the numbers: budgets, financial documents, operating costs, public records, statistical series, grant rolls, and reported outcomes. We use those results to guide analysis. We do not use them to decorate a predetermined argument.

This document is the canonical, repo-side version of the methodology published on the live portal at [bitcoinforthearts.org/research/methodology](https://bitcoinforthearts.org/research/methodology). The two are kept in sync.

> **The standard, in one sentence.** A claim is ready for publication when a competent third party — a journalist, foundation officer, professor, or auditor — can follow the citation to a primary source, find the exact data, and reproduce our reading of it.

---

## Research principles

1. **Primary sources first.** Official source material is preferred over summaries, commentary, or social media posts.
2. **No unsourced statistics.** A number without a primary source is not ready for publication.
3. **No hype.** Reports avoid advocacy language, partisan framing, and inflated claims. The evidence carries the argument.
4. **Numbers are treated seriously.** Budgets, costs, expenses, grants, revenue, and balance sheets are among the strongest guides to institutional reality. We treat them as such.
5. **Limitations are explicit.** Preliminary, proposed, lagged, revised, or estimated data is labeled. We disclose what we do not know.
6. **Institutional byline.** Reports are published under "Bitcoin for the Arts Research." The byline is a standard, not a person.

---

## Source hierarchy

We prefer sources higher on this hierarchy. Lower tiers are used to provide live context — case studies, institutional quotes, local examples — when higher-tier evidence is unavailable or incomplete.

### Tier 1 — Budgets, financial statements, and operating documents

Official budgets, appropriations, operating expenses, balance sheets, audited statements, IRS Form 990 filings, grant guidelines, grant rolls, and agency annual reports. **Preferred whenever available.**

### Tier 2 — Government and statistical agencies

Federal, state, local, and international statistical agencies for labor, price, GDP, monetary, demographic, and public-finance data. Examples:

- U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) — including the *Arts and Cultural Production Satellite Account* (ACPSA)
- U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) — including the *American Time Use Survey*
- U.S. Department of the Treasury — including *State and Local Fiscal Recovery Funds* reporting
- National Endowment for the Arts (NEA) — *Research & Analysis* publications, grant rolls, ARP records
- National Assembly of State Arts Agencies (NASAA) — state appropriation series
- Internal Revenue Service — Form 990 / 990-PF public filings
- International equivalents (Eurostat, UNESCO Institute for Statistics, OECD cultural statistics, national ministries of culture)

### Tier 3 — Peer-reviewed and university research

Academic research where methods are transparent and the research question matches the claim being made.

### Tier 4 — Major nonprofit research organizations

Sector research from established nonprofit, philanthropic, and arts-policy institutions: Brookings, Pew Research, Candid / Foundation Center, Americans for the Arts, the Urban Institute's National Center for Charitable Statistics, regional arts organizations (Arts Midwest, Mid Atlantic Arts, etc.), and equivalents abroad.

### Tier 5 — Reputable journalism and institutional reporting

Used for live case studies, institutional quotes, and local examples when primary documents are unavailable or incomplete. Always paired with a primary-source citation when the journalism itself relies on one.

## What does not count as evidence

- A number quoted on social media without a primary source
- A figure that appears only in a press release without underlying documentation
- A claim generated by an AI tool without independent source verification
- A round number cited "according to industry estimates" without a named, locatable estimate
- An older statistic presented as current data without an explicit "as of" date

When the only available source falls into one of the categories above, we either (a) hold the claim until a primary source is found, (b) publish it as a labeled estimate with the methodology disclosed, or (c) drop the claim.

---

## AI, analysis, and human review

AI tools may assist with:

- Searching for and retrieving documents
- Summarizing long primary sources
- Drafting outlines and prose
- Building draft tables, charts, and data extractions for human verification
- Translating non-English source material as a working draft (always with human review of the final cite)

AI output **is not treated as evidence.** Every factual claim must be traceable to a reviewed primary source before publication. Claims generated by AI without source verification are excluded or held for further review.

### AI-generated imagery

We may use AI-generated imagery to depict already-verified data — hero illustrations, section headers, motif illustrations, chart-explainer images. AI imagery is never used to manufacture or imply data that has not been independently verified through the source hierarchy above. AI-generated images are labeled as such in the relevant report or media folder README.

## Review cadence

| Document type | Cadence |
|---|---|
| **Annual flagship report** (e.g. *State of Arts Funding 20XX*) | Reviewed and updated annually; Q2 publication target |
| **Deep-dive reports** | Reviewed semiannually, or sooner if a primary source materially changes the analysis |
| **All reports** | Each report displays a "last reviewed" date so readers can understand the currency of the evidence |
| **Datasets** (`data/`) | Refreshed when the underlying source publishes a new release; versioned in git history |

---

## Corrections policy

Errors will happen. When they do, we fix them publicly.

1. **Open an issue** on [GitHub](https://github.com/Bitcoin-For-The-Arts/Research/issues) or email `info@bitcoinforthearts.org` with the report title, the claim, the source, and any page/table/line reference.
2. We acknowledge receipt and assess the correction against the source hierarchy.
3. If the correction stands, we update the report, update the "last reviewed" date, and note the change in the report's revision history.
4. Prior versions remain accessible in git history.

We do not silently overwrite. The expectation is that a journalist or researcher who cited an earlier version can find what changed and when.

## Versioning

| Version | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **v1** | First publication; meets the citation standard above |
| **v1.x** | Same scope, refined language, tightened sources, additional citations, or new chart data |
| **v2** | Substantive expansion of scope, new sections, or annual-cycle re-issue |
