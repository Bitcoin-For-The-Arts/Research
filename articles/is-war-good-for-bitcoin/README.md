# Is War Good for Bitcoin? — editorial article

**Byline:** Bitcoin for the Arts Research
**Channel:** Substack
**Status:** Editorial framing — monetary debasement and the funding of the arts
**Read time:** ~9–10 minutes (~2,000 words)

## Abstract

A historical-monetary editorial tracing how war has been funded by currency debasement from Ancient Rome's denarius through World War I, World War II, Vietnam, and Iraq/Afghanistan — and what that pattern reveals about the monetary system arts institutions and working artists depend on. Concludes with the structural argument that connects wartime debasement to the present-day arts funding crisis: arts cuts and war-time inflation are downstream of the same monetary mechanism. Companion editorial to *Sound Money for the Arts*.

## Headline figures (with sourcing notes — to be hardened in the canonical report)

- **74.6% debt + 7% money creation** — U.S. financing of World War I (cf. NBER and Treasury historical studies on war finance)
- **46% debt + 10.1% money creation** — U.S. financing of World War II
- **Negative real returns on federal war bonds** — WWII U.S. government bondholder outcome (NBER research on inflation as repayment mechanism)
- **From >50% to 19%** — Arts Council England share of The Royal Ballet's revenue (illustrative reference, to be sourced to ACE annual reports in the canonical report)

These figures are accurate in the editorial register; in the canonical *Sound Money for the Arts* report they will be replaced with footnoted, page-cited references to NBER, Treasury, BLS, FRED, and Arts Council annual reports. See [`CITATIONS.md`](../../CITATIONS.md).

## Scope note

Per [`ETHOS.md`](../../ETHOS.md), this article analyzes **Bitcoin as monetary technology only** — fixed-supply sound money, sanctions resistance, and the moral case for transparent war-cost accounting. It does not analyze, advocate for, or compare to other cryptocurrencies, tokens, NFTs, ordinals, or any non-monetary use of the Bitcoin network. The wartime-adoption examples (Russia–Ukraine cross-border transfers, Canadian protest accounts, Lebanon/Argentina/Nigeria/Turkey collapsing-currency adoption) are referenced as monetary phenomena.
