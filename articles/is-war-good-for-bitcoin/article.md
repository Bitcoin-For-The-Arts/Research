# Is War Good for Bitcoin?

*Wars are funded by currency debasement. Currency debasement is the reason Bitcoin exists. What does that mean for how we think about both?*

**Byline:** Bitcoin for the Arts Research
**Channel:** Substack
**Status:** Editorial framing
**Related report:** [`reports/sound-money-for-the-arts/`](../../reports/sound-money-for-the-arts/)

---

Throughout history, war has been funded by destroying the value of money.

This is not opinion. It is a pattern so consistent across civilizations and centuries that it functions as something closer to a law of economics: when nations go to war, they inflate their currency to pay for it, and the people who can least afford it bear the greatest cost.

Ancient Rome debased the denarius — reducing its silver content to fund military expansion — until the currency collapsed and Emperor Diocletian resorted to price controls that failed spectacularly. Britain entered World War I with the pound sterling as the world's reserve currency and exited with an empire in irreversible decline. The United States financed World War I through 74.6% debt and 7% direct money creation. World War II was funded through 46% debt and 10.1% money creation. Vietnam was financed through tax increases and expansionary monetary policy that produced the stagflation of the 1970s — a decade of rising prices and stagnant wages that crushed working families. The Iraq and Afghanistan wars were funded almost entirely through debt, adding trillions to the national balance sheet with costs that are still compounding.

In every case, the pattern is identical: governments spend more than they have, print or borrow the difference, and the resulting inflation quietly transfers wealth from those without assets to those who hold them. The soldier returns home to prices that rose while they were gone. The artist finds that the grant they were promised buys less than it did when it was offered. The family that saved diligently discovers their savings purchase fewer groceries each month.

War is an inflation machine. And inflation is a tax that no one votes for.

---

Now consider Bitcoin.

Bitcoin's supply is fixed at 21 million. It cannot be inflated by any government, central bank, or wartime emergency. No president can print more of it. No treasury secretary can issue Bitcoin bonds. No emergency act can authorize the creation of additional units to fund a military campaign.

If Bitcoin holds true to its principle — a non-inflationary sound money standard — then the economic consequences of war-driven currency debasement do not apply to those who hold it. The currency used to fund the war loses purchasing power. Bitcoin, by design, does not.

This is not a political statement. It is a mathematical one. A currency with an expanding supply loses value per unit over time. A currency with a fixed supply does not. When a government inflates its money to fund a war, it is choosing to debase the savings of its citizens. Bitcoin offers an alternative that no government controls.

The question, then, is not whether Bitcoin benefits from war. The question is what war reveals about the monetary systems we already live under.

---

If these two principles hold — that inflationary currencies lose value during wartime spending, and that a fixed-supply currency preserves value through the same period — then the question becomes deeply personal.

How do I value war? Which of these two monetary principles do I align with? What is my criteria for necessitating war? What is my moral stance on the funding of it?

One could reasonably ask: why should I even consider these questions? Most wars are initiated by the state without the meaningful consent of its citizens, usually under the broad justification of national defense. The average person has no vote on whether to go to war, no say in how it is financed, and no control over the inflation that follows.

But the lack of control does not provide immunity from the consequences. Whether or not you consented to the war, you will pay for it — through the slow, silent erosion of your currency's purchasing power. Every dollar printed to fund a conflict is a dollar that makes your savings, your wages, and your time worth less.

This is not a burden shared equally. Those who hold assets — real estate, equities, commodities — see their holdings rise in nominal terms as the currency inflates. Those who hold cash, who earn hourly wages, who depend on fixed incomes — artists, teachers, service workers, the elderly — absorb the full weight of the debasement.

War widens the wealth gap. Not through conquest, but through the money printer.

---

The data bears this out.

During World War II, the U.S. government delivered large negative real returns to federal bondholders — meaning that people who lent money to the government through war bonds received less purchasing power in return than they gave. Research from the National Bureau of Economic Research confirms that inflation, not default, was the mechanism: the government repaid its debts in dollars that bought less.

A study from the Institute for Economics and Peace found that major wars consistently produce adverse supply shocks — destroying capital, disrupting trade, and diverting labor from civilian to military use. The result: GDP falls, inflation rises, and the recovery takes years. Their research across multiple conflicts found persistent reductions in real GDP, household consumption, investment, exports, and government revenue, paired with sustained increases in inflation.

These are not abstractions. These are the conditions that close theaters, defund arts councils, cancel grants, and force artists to choose between their craft and survival.

---

There is another dimension worth examining.

In an era of accelerating technological change, the traditional tools of economic statecraft are losing their edge. Sanctions — historically one of the primary alternatives to armed conflict — depend on the sanctioned nation using the same financial system as the sanctioning one. If a nation adopts a monetary system outside the control of any single entity or centralized coalition, the leverage of sanctions diminishes.

Bitcoin is that system. It operates on a decentralized network that no government controls. It does not require permission from a bank, compliance with a sanctions list, or approval from a correspondent institution. It simply works — for anyone, anywhere, at any time.

If nations or populations begin adopting such a system at scale — whether out of necessity during conflict, as a hedge against sanctions, or simply because the existing system no longer serves them — the result could be an unlikely but accelerating path to mass adoption of a decentralized monetary standard. Not driven by ideology, but by survival.

This has already begun. During the Russia–Ukraine conflict, both sides used Bitcoin for cross-border transfers when traditional banking failed. When the Canadian government froze bank accounts of protest participants in 2022, Bitcoin provided a financial channel that could not be shut off. In countries with collapsing currencies — Lebanon, Argentina, Nigeria, Turkey — Bitcoin adoption has grown not because people read white papers, but because their money stopped working.

War, sanctions, and economic instability do not make Bitcoin "good." They make Bitcoin necessary.

---

So: is war good for Bitcoin?

In the narrow, mechanical sense — yes. War-driven inflation highlights exactly the problem Bitcoin was designed to solve. Every dollar printed to fund a conflict is an advertisement for a monetary system that cannot be debased. Every frozen bank account is a case study in why permissionless money matters. Every sanctions regime that fails because the target moved to decentralized rails is evidence that the old financial order is losing its grip.

But there is a deeper question here, and it is a moral one.

If Bitcoin benefits from the conditions that war creates — inflation, instability, loss of trust in institutions — then do Bitcoiners have an incentive to welcome war? Should they?

No.

The entire philosophical foundation of Bitcoin is the preservation of individual sovereignty — the right to hold the product of your labor in a form that cannot be confiscated, inflated, or controlled. That principle is fundamentally at odds with war, which is the ultimate violation of individual sovereignty. War takes lives, displaces families, destroys communities, and conscripts human beings into violence they did not choose.

Bitcoin does not need war to succeed. It needs time. It needs adoption. It needs artists and builders and communities using it to create value — not destroy it. The conditions that drive Bitcoin adoption during wartime are the same conditions that crush the most vulnerable people on earth.

The moral position is clear: Bitcoin is a tool for building a world where war becomes harder to fund, not a tool that profits from war's destruction. A world on a Bitcoin standard — where governments cannot print money to finance military campaigns — is a world where the true cost of war must be borne transparently, through direct taxation, with the full knowledge and consent of the people being asked to pay.

That transparency alone might be the most powerful antiwar force ever created.

---

At Bitcoin For The Arts, we see this through the lens of what we know: the creative economy. Arts funding in America is already in crisis. The National Endowment for the Arts faces elimination. Arts Council funding for institutions like The Royal Ballet has dropped from over 50% to 19%. Grants are being canceled. Programs are being cut.

These cuts are not caused by war alone — but they are caused by the same underlying force: governments that have spent beyond their means, inflated their currencies to cover the gap, and are now forced to cut the programs that serve the most vulnerable to pay for the debts they've accumulated.

Sound money changes this equation. A Bitcoin endowment does not lose purchasing power to inflation. A grant paid in Bitcoin does not buy less next year than it does today. An artist who saves in Bitcoin is not slowly robbed by a monetary policy designed to benefit borrowers at the expense of savers.

We are not building Bitcoin For The Arts because we believe war is coming. We are building it because the consequences of the wars already fought — and the money already printed to pay for them — are here. And artists, as always, are among the first to feel it.

The sovereign renaissance is not a response to war. It is a response to the monetary system that makes war so easy to fund and so hard to stop.
