# My High School Built a 501(c)(3) Inside a Public School in 1979. I'm Building One on Bitcoin in 2026.

*From the Baltimore School for the Arts to Bitcoin For The Arts — same workaround, new rails.*

In 1979, a group of Baltimore civic leaders did something almost no one had attempted before. New York City had already built public arts high schools — the High School of Music & Art back in 1936, then the High School of Performing Arts in 1948, both of which would eventually merge into LaGuardia. Public arts education at the secondary level was not a new idea. But what Baltimore did was structurally different. The Baltimore School for the Arts was built inside the public school system, but with a 501(c)(3) foundation built inside the school. The foundation was the load-bearing piece. Without it, BSA would have been just another underfunded city school with a couple of dance studios and a piano. With it, it became one of the best secondary arts conservatories in the United States — a school whose graduates dance with Alvin Ailey, perform on Broadway, exhibit at the Whitney, and play for the Baltimore Symphony.

I know, because I am one of them. Class of '96, dance. The school changed the trajectory of my life. And the founders of that school changed the trajectory of mine without ever knowing my name.

This week, I read [a piece in Bmore Art](https://bmoreart.com/2026/04/baltimore-school-for-the-arts-from-an-iconic-legacy-to-the-next-generation.html) about the school's transition into its next generation of leadership. The article got me thinking about what those founders actually did — Margaret DeMan Armstrong, the long-time Baltimore arts champion and teacher whose concept it was in the first place; Mark Joseph, then president of the Board of School Commissioners; Mayor William Donald Schaefer; Tony Cary, the first chair of the school's Board of Overseers; David Simon, the founding director Cary recruited from the Manhattan School of Music. They were not just running a school. They were running a structural workaround. And the workaround is the thing I want to talk about, because it's the same workaround I'm building right now at Bitcoin For The Arts.

## What the Baltimore School for the Arts founders actually did

Strip away the prestige. Strip away the famous alumni. Look at the structural problem they were solving in the mid-1970s, when the conversation about creating the school began.

Baltimore at that moment had a school system in crisis — 135,000 students, 12,000 employees, a public board that had just fired the city's first Black superintendent, news cameras at every meeting. The institutional structure was failing the kids it was supposed to serve. Margaret Armstrong, a long-time Baltimore teacher and arts champion, had been pushing the idea of a city arts school for years. In 1971, then-superintendent Dr. Roland Patterson authorized her to form a committee to work toward it. She used Title I funding to build a cultural enrichment program — exposing city public school students to professional artistic performance — that became the proof-of-concept for what the school could be. The idea was hers. The school exists because she would not let it die.

But what Armstrong and the others wanted to build was structurally impossible inside a normal public school. Public schools have to be everything to every student. Public schools are constrained by district-wide budget formulas, union work rules, curriculum standards, political pressure to spread resources thin. A public school cannot, on its own, do what an arts conservatory needs to do — hire professional artists as faculty, pay them more than the union pay scale, audition students for admission, raise private dollars for instruments and equipment, and operate with the freedom of a private institution while staying free for every student who walks in.

The earlier public arts schools in New York had each found their own version of this problem and solved it imperfectly — they were specialized but still bound by most of the constraints of the regular system. Baltimore's founders studied that model and decided to go further. They invented a hybrid. The school is part of the Baltimore City Public Schools system — students pay nothing, audition is the only barrier, and Baltimore kids get priority. But the school's *governance* and its *funding* were carved out into a separate structure: a Board of Trustees, a director with an arts background instead of a traditional administrator, the freedom to hire and pay artist-faculty outside the union pay scale, and — critically — an **independent 501(c)(3) foundation that today provides roughly one-third of the school's funding.**

That foundation was the load-bearing piece. The school could not have done what it did without it. The public dollars paid for the basics. The private 501(c)(3) paid for the excellence.

David Simon ran that hybrid for sixteen years. Tony Cary recruited him from the Manhattan School of Music — a credible national arts name, brought to Baltimore to be the standard-setter for what the school would become. Simon was a composer, a musician, an arts administrator with a national reputation, and as far as I can tell, the right kind of stubborn. He set a standard of excellence in both arts and academics that became the school's identity. He built the academic program into one of the strongest in Baltimore — not just an arts school with academics tacked on, but a school where the academics earned their own respect. He passed away in 2017, but the blueprint he ran for sixteen years is the blueprint the school still operates on today.

I owe him my career. Most of my classmates owe him their careers. And the dancers who come through Baltimore School for the Arts in 2026 are still benefiting from the structural decisions he and the original founders made fifty years ago.

## The same workaround, in a different system

When I founded Bitcoin For The Arts in 2025, I was not consciously thinking about David Simon. I was thinking about the conversation I keep having with working artists — that arts funding in America is collapsing, that the NEA has been targeted for elimination in two consecutive federal budgets, that San Diego just cut city arts funding by 86 percent in a single budget cycle, that state arts appropriations are projected to drop 7.7 percent nationally. The institutional model is failing, the way Baltimore's school system was failing in the mid-1970s.

But the more I built BFTA, the more I realized: I am running the exact same kind of workaround.

The traditional arts funding system in America is the public school system of cultural philanthropy. It is too big, too constrained, too political, too tied to budget formulas that no longer add up. Foundations chase the same prestige projects. Government grants come and go with administrations. Endowments are denominated in a currency that loses purchasing power year after year. The system can no longer reliably fund the artists it claims to serve.

So I am building a hybrid, the way Mark Joseph and David Simon built a hybrid. Bitcoin For The Arts is a 501(c)(3) — registered, transparent, governed by a board, accountable to the public allocation we publish on our website. It looks, on paper, like every other arts nonprofit. But the load-bearing piece — the part that makes the structure actually work — is a different rail underneath.

We pay artists in Bitcoin. We hold our reserve in Bitcoin. The Bitcoin and Lightning Network make it possible to pay an artist anywhere in the world in seconds, with no platform fees, with no permission, with no third party between donor and artist. Our HODL Vault — the long-term endowment reserve we are currently establishing — is built in a currency with a fixed supply, which means a $500 grant denominated in BTC today does not lose purchasing power over the decades a foundation operates. That single structural choice changes the math of an arts endowment in a way no traditional foundation has access to.

This is what David Simon and the BSA founders did fifty years ago, in a different domain. They built a funding mechanism inside an institutional system that should have made their work impossible, and the funding mechanism is what made the institution actually work. The 501(c)(3) foundation inside the public school is what allowed the school to be excellent. The Bitcoin rails inside the 501(c)(3) is what will allow Bitcoin For The Arts to be sustainable at scale.

## What I'm taking from the playbook

The Baltimore School for the Arts founders did not have a model to copy. There was no successful public arts conservatory of their kind in 1979. They had to invent the structure as they built it, defend it from a school system that did not understand it, and convince donors and parents and city officials to trust something that did not yet exist.

That is the founder experience. I recognize it because I am living it.

What I am taking from David Simon's blueprint is not the specific structure. It is the conviction that **when the institutional system can no longer fund what needs to exist, you do not wait for the institutional system to fix itself. You build the workaround. You build it inside the system if you can, alongside the system if you must, and you defend it long enough for it to start producing the results that make the case for itself.**

I do not have David Simon's classical music training. I do not have Mark Joseph's political relationships. I do not have the Mayor of Baltimore's office behind me. I have a 501(c)(3), a public allocation model, a global broadcast event in September, twenty-one featured artists who have agreed to contribute their time as mission supporters, a few extraordinary board members and advisors, an audience that grows every week, and a deep, patient conviction that Bitcoin is the right rail for the next century of arts patronage.

What I do have is the foundational lesson the BSA founders left behind: **build the workaround, defend it, and let the work speak for itself.**

If you are an artist whose career was changed by an institution that would not have existed if its founders had waited for permission — and that is most of us — you already understand exactly what we are doing at Bitcoin For The Arts.

We are taking the next page out of the playbook.
