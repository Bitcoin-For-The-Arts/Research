*San Diego just slashed arts funding by 86%. Across America, the pattern is the same. The old model is dying. Here's why Bitcoin is the only path forward for the arts.*

On April 15, 2026, San Diego Mayor Todd Gloria proposed cutting the city's arts funding from $13.8 million to under $2 million.

That is an 86% reduction. In one budget cycle.

The Organizational Support Program — the backbone of funding for San Diego's nonprofit arts groups — would be eliminated. Creative Communities San Diego — gone. Hundreds of local arts organizations that rely on city funding, and use it to secure matching grants from other sources, would be crippled. Christine Martinez, manager of Arts and Culture San Diego, called the proposed cuts "decimating" and warned of "long-term consequences for San Diego's economy and identity."

This is not an isolated incident. This is a pattern. And it is accelerating.

---

## The Collapse Is Happening Everywhere

San Diego's arts cuts are part of a $146 million deficit the city is trying to close — driven by employee pay raises the city agreed to in 2023, sluggish growth in hotel tax revenue, sales tax, and property tax, and the voters' rejection of a sales tax hike in November 2024. The arts were the easiest thing to cut. They always are.

But zoom out and the picture gets worse.

At the federal level, the Trump administration proposed eliminating the National Endowment for the Arts entirely in its FY2026 budget. In May 2025, the NEA began mass termination of grants — withdrawing and canceling hundreds of pending and existing awards to arts organizations across the country. The House proposed a 35% cut. Congress ultimately kept the agency alive with level funding for FY2026, but the administration came back in April 2026 with another proposal to eliminate it in FY2027.

The NEA is still standing. For now. But the message to arts organizations is unmistakable: do not depend on this money.

At the state level, total legislative appropriations for state arts agencies are projected to decrease 7.7% in FY2026 — from $704.3 million to $650.2 million, according to the National Assembly of State Arts Agencies. Twenty-one states are projecting decreases. California alone is looking at a 40.8% drop. Missouri: 59.7%. Minnesota: 22%.

And these numbers land on top of decades of erosion. Even the most prestigious cultural institutions in the world are not immune. Public arts funding for major ballet companies, opera houses, and performing arts centers across Europe has been declining for years — organizations that once relied on government support for the majority of their revenue are now scrambling to fill widening gaps with ticket sales and private donors.

This is not a blip. This is a structural failure.

---

## Why This Keeps Happening

The reason arts funding is always first on the chopping block is not because art doesn't matter. It's because of how governments fund themselves.

Cities, states, and the federal government operate on fiat currency — money that can be printed, borrowed against, and inflated. When revenues don't meet expenses (which is most of the time), governments borrow. When borrowing gets too expensive, they cut. And when they cut, they start with the things that don't have a political constituency powerful enough to fight back.

Police, fire, and transportation have powerful unions and immediate public safety arguments. Arts organizations have passionate advocates but small budgets and no 911 calls. So the arts get cut. Every time.

But the deeper problem is the monetary system itself. San Diego's $146 million deficit didn't appear overnight. It's the result of years of spending commitments made in an inflationary environment — 23% pay raises for city workers funded by revenue projections that assumed perpetual growth. When hotel tax revenue dropped 3% instead of rising 6%, the whole house of cards collapsed.

This is what fiat money does. It incentivizes governments to make promises they can't keep, funded by revenue that depends on an economy that never stops growing. When growth slows — and it always does — the promises break. And the arts are the first promise broken.

Inflation compounds the damage. Every dollar the NEA distributes buys less than it did the year before. Every city arts grant covers fewer programs, fewer artists, fewer productions. The funding is shrinking in nominal terms and in real terms simultaneously.

The old model doesn't need reform. It needs replacement.

## The Numbers Tell the Story

Let's put this in perspective.

San Diego's arts funding is dropping from $13.8 million to $2 million. That $11.8 million cut will be redirected to cover other city expenses. The arts community will lose the money, and they'll also lose the matching grants that the city funding helped them secure from other sources. The multiplier effect means the real damage is far greater than $11.8 million.

State arts funding nationally is projected to drop $54 million in a single year — from $704 million to $650 million.

The NEA's entire annual budget is $207 million. For context, that is roughly what the federal government spends every 22 minutes.

These are the numbers that the entire American arts ecosystem depends on. And they are vanishing.
